## Evaluation techniques


### 1. Systematic observation `TEC_OBS_SIS`

_Planned and documented observation of performance during an activity._

**Phase:** Proceso · **Participation:** Docente · **Modality:** Presencial · **Location:** Aula / Laboratorio / Exterior · **Grouping:** Individual / Gran grupo · **AI resistance:** Alta

_Keywords: observation, performance, process_

As a technique in the strict sense, this element describes a procedure for gathering evidence. It can be combined with different purposes, agents and methodological contexts.

Systematic observation is a technique in which the teacher plans in advance what to observe, at which moments and with which recording instrument. It is not a matter of looking in a generic way, but of attending to specific, observable criteria while students are performing.

How to apply it: before the session, the indicators to be observed are defined (e.g. 'uses laboratory materials correctly', 'argues with evidence', 'listens actively'). During the activity, the teacher uses a checklist, observation scale or rubric to record what they see. At the end, this information is used to give feedback, adjust teaching or contribute to grading.

It is especially useful in practical activities, debates, oral presentations, workshops and cooperative work, where the evidence of learning occurs in real time and is not captured in a physical product.

**Purpose:** Record actions, procedures, participation and habits.

**When to use:** When evidence occurs in real time and reviewing a final product is not enough.

**Typical evidence:** Oral performances, practical work, group work, participation.

**Limitations:** Can be subjective if there are no clear criteria.

### 2. Incidental observation `TEC_OBS_INC`

_Recording of relevant events that arise unexpectedly._

**Phase:** Proceso · **Participation:** Docente · **Modality:** Presencial · **Location:** Aula / Laboratorio / Exterior · **Grouping:** Individual / Gran grupo · **AI resistance:** Alta

_Keywords: observation, incidental_

As a technique in the strict sense, this element describes a procedure for gathering evidence. It can be combined with different purposes, agents and methodological contexts.

Incidental observation records relevant events that emerge spontaneously during everyday classroom activity, without the teacher having anticipated them. A perceptive comment, a recurring difficulty, a group conflict or an unexpected initiative are all examples of incidental evidence.

How to apply it: the teacher should always have an anecdotal record, a notebook or an application to hand in which to briefly note the date, the student's name and the observed event. These notes, accumulated over time, reveal patterns of behaviour, progress or difficulties that planned observation might not capture.

It does not replace systematic observation, but complements it by providing unexpected evidence and qualitative insights into the learning process.

**Purpose:** Detect progress, difficulties or significant incidents.

**When to use:** When an unforeseen but relevant piece of evidence appears.

**Typical evidence:** Behaviours, comments, conflicts, initiatives, doubts.

**Limitations:** Does not replace systematic observation.

**Active techniques using this:** What makes you say that?, Game-Based Learning (GBL), Problem-Based Learning (PBL), Experiential Learning, Metacognition Ladder

### 3. Analysis of productions `TEC_PROD`

_Assessment of products created by students._

**Phase:** Proceso/Final · **Participation:** Docente · **Modality:** Presencial / Online · **Location:** Aula / Virtual / Domicilio · **Grouping:** Individual / Grupo pequeño · **AI resistance:** Media

_Keywords: production, product_

As a technique in the strict sense, this element describes a procedure for gathering evidence. It can be combined with different purposes, agents and methodological contexts.

The analysis of productions consists in assessing the artefacts or products created by students: written texts, reports, projects, presentations, videos, concept maps, scale models, portfolios or any other tangible or digital product.

How to apply it: the criteria to be assessed and the instrument to be used (rubric, correction guide, descriptive scale) are defined in advance. The review can be done outside class time, which allows the teacher to dedicate more time and attention than during live observation. It is essential to return written feedback that explains not only the level achieved, but what can be improved and how.

When the final product does not reflect the full learning (because the process was complex or the work was done in a group), it is advisable to complement it with process monitoring, drafts or brief interviews.

**Purpose:** Assess quality, content, structure, process and transfer.

**When to use:** When there is an assessable product or artefact.

**Typical evidence:** Texts, reports, projects, videos, maps, infographics, portfolios.

**Limitations:** Does not show the full process if not accompanied by ongoing monitoring.

### 4. Specific tests `TEC_PRUEBA`

_Situations specifically designed to check learning._

**Phase:** Inicial/Proceso/Final · **Participation:** Docente · **Modality:** Presencial / Online · **Location:** Aula / Virtual · **Grouping:** Individual · **AI resistance:** Media

_Keywords: test, check_

As a technique in the strict sense, this element describes a procedure for gathering evidence. It can be combined with different purposes, agents and methodological contexts.

Specific tests are situations specifically designed to check learning under controlled conditions. They include written development tests, multiple choice tests, short answer tests, oral tests and practical tests.

How to apply them: the design must be aligned with the assessment criteria and the knowledge covered. Each question or task should be linked to a specific criterion. It is recommended to share the correction criteria in advance (or at least the type of task) so that students can prepare in an oriented way.

They are useful for accrediting achievements and comparing results, but should not be the only assessment instrument. Combined with other evidence (productions, observation, self-assessment), they offer a more complete and fair picture of learning.

**Purpose:** Check knowledge, application, reasoning or skills.

**When to use:** When a specific or comparable check is needed.

**Typical evidence:** Written, oral or practical responses under controlled conditions.

**Limitations:** Can reduce assessment if used as the only evidence.

**Active techniques using this:** Inquiry-Based Learning, Question-Based Learning (QBL), Problem-Based Learning (PBL), Challenge-Based Learning (ChBL), Chain of Questions, Design Thinking, Generate Hypotheses, Generate Solutions, Shared Reading, STAD (Student Teams Achievement Divisions), Teams-Games-Tournament (TGT), One for All, Understanding by Design (UbD), TPACK, The 4Cs of Learning, Predict, Observe, Explain (POE), Worked examples, Mastery learning, Hinge questions, Showdown, Computational thinking

### 5. Oral exchanges `TEC_ORAL`

_Gathering evidence through oral communication._

**Phase:** Proceso/Final · **Participation:** Docente/Iguales · **Modality:** Presencial / Online · **Location:** Aula / Virtual · **Grouping:** Individual / Gran grupo · **AI resistance:** Alta

_Keywords: orality, communication_

As a technique in the strict sense, this element describes a procedure for gathering evidence. It can be combined with different purposes, agents and methodological contexts.

Oral exchanges are situations in which the teacher gathers learning evidence through oral communication: questions to the group, guided debates, colloquia, individual interviews or project defences.

How to apply them: it is advisable to plan the questions or script before the session and have a recording instrument ready (oral rubric, observation scale, checklist). If the exchange is a group activity, it is difficult to observe everyone at once; the focus of attention can be alternated or the session recorded for later review (with appropriate permissions).

They allow for the assessment of deep comprehension, argumentation, synthesis ability, fluency and oral expression — aspects that a written test does not capture. They also allow the teacher to ask follow-up questions to explore underlying reasoning.

**Purpose:** Assess comprehension, argumentation, expression and dialogue.

**When to use:** When assessing communication, verbal reasoning or interaction.

**Typical evidence:** Contributions, responses, debates, defences, interviews.

### 6. Probing and questioning `TEC_SONDEO`

_Quick collection of responses, prior ideas or comprehension through brief questions._

**Phase:** Inicial/Proceso/Final · **Participation:** Docente/Alumno · **Modality:** Presencial / Online · **Location:** Aula / Virtual · **Grouping:** Individual / Gran grupo · **AI resistance:** Alta

_Keywords: questions, probing, diagnostic, check, exit_

As a technique in the strict sense, probing and questioning consists of gathering evidence through brief questions, oral or written, designed to make visible what students know, believe they know, do not understand or have just learned.

How to apply it: the teacher poses a question, a KPSI, an exit ticket, a brainstorming activity or a brief questionnaire. Responses are recorded with a simple instrument and analysed to decide whether to move forward, review, regroup, clarify or provide feedback.

It is especially useful in diagnostic and formative assessment, because it provides quick, actionable information. It should not be confused with a long test nor used only for grading.

**Purpose:** Explore prior knowledge, check immediate comprehension and detect adjustment needs.

**When to use:** When quick information is needed to start, adjust or close a session without turning the activity into an extensive test.

**Typical evidence:** Brief responses, oral questions, initial questionnaires, KPSI, exit tickets, brainstorming.

**Limitations:** Can yield superficial evidence if questions are not well formulated or are not used afterwards to make teaching decisions.

**Phase:** Proceso · **Participation:** Docente/Alumno/Iguales · **Modality:** Presencial / Online · **Location:** Aula / Virtual / Domicilio · **Grouping:** Individual · **AI resistance:** Baja

As a technique in the strict sense, the analysis of processes and reflections consists of reviewing evidence that shows how learning has developed: planning, drafts, decisions, difficulties, revisions, comments received and improvement commitments.

It is especially useful in projects, portfolios, cooperative work and complex tasks, because it allows assessment of the learning journey and guides improvement before the final submission.

**Purpose:** Understand how students learn, plan, revise, cooperate and improve during a task.

**When to use:** When it is important to assess learning during the process and not only the final product.

**Typical evidence:** Journals, logbooks, drafts, partial submissions, reflection cards, self-reports, peer reviews.

### 8. Evaluative interview `TEC_ENTREV_EVAL`

_Planned conversation to explore comprehension, reasoning or process._

**Phase:** Proceso/Final · **Participation:** Docente/Alumno · **Modality:** Presencial / Online · **Location:** Aula / Virtual · **Grouping:** Individual · **AI resistance:** Alta

_Keywords: interview, oral, inquiry_

The evaluative interview is a conversation with an assessment purpose, prepared with questions or observation focuses. It allows exploration of how the student reasons, what decisions they made and what understanding underpins a production.

How to apply it: a brief script is defined, responses are recorded with a guide or oral rubric and questions are kept comparable across students. It can be used in project defences, portfolios or review of complex work.

It is useful for obtaining deep evidence, but must be planned to avoid excessive improvisation or unfair differences between interviews.

**Purpose:** Delve into evidence that is not clear from a product or test.

**When to use:** When it is necessary to probe student reasoning or verify authorship and comprehension.

**Typical evidence:** Oral responses, explanations, arguments, process decisions.

**Limitations:** Time-consuming and requires a common script to ensure fairness.

**Active techniques using this:** Action-Based Learning (ABL), Challenge-Based Learning (ChBL), Design Thinking, Digital Storytelling, Interview, Think, Puzzle, Explore, Gamification

### 9. Survey and questionnaire `TEC_ENC_CUEST`

_Structured collection of responses through closed or open questions._

**Phase:** Inicial/Proceso/Final · **Participation:** Docente/Alumno · **Modality:** Presencial / Online · **Location:** Aula / Virtual / Domicilio · **Grouping:** Individual · **AI resistance:** Baja

_Keywords: survey, questionnaire, form_

Surveys and questionnaires collect responses in a structured way. They can be used for diagnostic, formative or summative purposes depending on the design of the questions.

How to apply them: clear items are drafted, it is decided whether responses will be anonymous or identified, and results are analysed to make teaching decisions. In assessment, it is important to distinguish between perception questionnaires and knowledge tests.

They are useful for their speed and comparability, but should not replace richer evidence when complex learning is being assessed.

**Purpose:** Obtain quick information about knowledge, perceptions, interests or monitoring.

**When to use:** When comparable and easy-to-analyse information is needed from the whole group.

**Typical evidence:** Questionnaires, forms, initial surveys, perception scales.

**Limitations:** Quality depends greatly on question formulation.

**Active techniques using this:** Research-Based Learning (RBL), Challenge-Based Learning (ChBL), Chain of Questions, Design Thinking, Flipped Classroom, KWL (I know, I want to know, I have learned), Compass Points, Minute Paper, Peer Instruction, 3-2-1 Bridge, Quiz-Quiz-Trade, Appointment Clock, Exit Ticket, Retrieval practice, Spaced practice

### 10. Documentary analysis `TEC_ANAL_DOC`

**Phase:** Proceso/Final · **Participation:** Docente · **Modality:** Presencial / Online · **Location:** Aula / Virtual / Domicilio · **Grouping:** Individual · **AI resistance:** Baja

Documentary analysis consists of reviewing documents and records generated during learning: notebooks, portfolios, minutes, edit histories or evidence repositories.

How to apply it: relevant documents are selected, contrasted with criteria or indicators, and care is taken to avoid assessing only the quantity of accumulated material. The evidence map helps identify which document supports each criterion.

It is a useful technique for continuous and criteria-based assessment, especially when the process leaves verifiable traces.

**Purpose:** Interpret already-produced information to assess progress, coherence or traceability.

**When to use:** When learning is documented in accumulated records or products.

**Typical evidence:** Notebooks, portfolios, minutes, revision histories, evidence banks.

**Limitations:** Can overvalue what is documented and leave out unrecorded actions.

### 11. Analysis of digital interactions `TEC_INTER_DIG`

_Interpretation of participation, traces and collaboration in digital environments._

**Phase:** Proceso · **Participation:** Docente/Alumno/Iguales · **Modality:** Online · **Location:** Virtual · **Grouping:** Individual / Gran grupo · **AI resistance:** Media

_Keywords: digital, interaction, traces, collaboration_

The analysis of digital interactions studies how students participate in digital spaces: contributions to shared documents, forums, comments, submissions, reviews and activity on platforms.

How to apply it: it is decided which traces are relevant to the criteria, histories or dashboards are reviewed and results are triangulated with products and teacher observation. Not all digital activity implies learning, so it must be interpreted with caution.

It is especially useful in collaborative projects and digital portfolios.

**Purpose:** Assess participation, contribution and process on platforms or shared documents.

**Typical evidence:** Forums, comments, edit histories, analytics, messages and digital submissions.

**Limitations:** Digital traces require pedagogical interpretation and respect for privacy.

**Active techniques using this:** Word-Phrase-Sentence, Community of Inquiry

### 12. Evidence triangulation `TEC_TRIANG`

_Contrasting several pieces of evidence to make a more robust assessment decision._

**Phase:** Proceso/Final · **Participation:** Docente · **Modality:** Presencial / Online · **Location:** Aula / Virtual · **Grouping:** Individual / Grupo pequeño · **AI resistance:** Alta

_Keywords: triangulation, evidence, validity_

Evidence triangulation consists of contrasting several sources before making a decision: observation, production, test, interview, self-assessment or process record.

How to apply it: identify which criterion is to be assessed, what evidence demonstrates it and whether the evidence is sufficient and consistent with each other. If evidence is inconsistent, analyse which is more valid or whether it is worth gathering additional evidence.

It is key in competency-based and criteria-based assessment, because it avoids making decisions based on a single isolated task.

**Purpose:** Increase validity and reliability by combining products, observations, tests and reflections.

**When to use:** When a single piece of evidence is insufficient to assess a criterion or competency.

**Typical evidence:** Rubrics, records, tests, portfolios, interviews, self-assessments.

**Limitations:** Requires deciding which evidence carries more weight and avoiding duplication of the same evidence.

**Active techniques using this:** Project-Based Learning (PjBL), Design Thinking, Compass Points, The Box, Service-Learning, Constructive alignment

## Observable evidence


### 1. Individual classroom work `EVI_TRAB_IND`

_Observation of the student working individually on tasks in class._

**Type:** Observation · **Phase:** Proceso · **Participation:** Docente · **Complexity:** Baja/Media · **Modality:** Presencial · **Location:** Aula · **Grouping:** Individual · **AI resistance:** Alta

_Keywords: individual work, classroom observation, autonomy_

Individual classroom work refers to any task the student performs alone during class time: exercises, readings, responses, problems, etc. The teacher can observe the process (how the student approaches the task) and the product (the completed work).

How to use it for assessment: combined with an observation guide or anecdotal record, it allows collecting information about pace, autonomy, persistence and problem-solving strategies.

An e-portfolio with selected evidence is not simply a folder of works: it requires students to choose evidence, explain why it demonstrates specific criteria and reflect on their progress.

### 84. Field notebook `EVI_CUAD_CAMPO`

_Record of observations, data and reflections during outdoor or real-world activities._

**Type:** Written/observational production · **Phase:** Proceso · **Participation:** Docente/Alumno · **Complexity:** Media · **Modality:** Presencial · **Location:** Outdoors / Laboratory / Classroom · **Grouping:** Individual / Small group · **AI resistance:** Alta

_Keywords: field notebook, outdoor, observation, nature_

The field notebook is the record produced by the student during activities carried out outside the classroom: field trips, visits, environmental studies or activities in natural spaces. Unlike the classroom notebook (EVI_CUADERNO), it documents real-time observations in uncontrolled contexts; unlike the laboratory report (EVI_INFO_LAB), it does not follow a closed experimental structure, but collects notes, sketches, measurements and on-the-spot reflections.

How to use it for assessment: the student records direct observations, measured data, drawings or sketches, emerging questions and initial reflections. The teacher assesses the accuracy and completeness of observations, the ability to record relevant data, and the quality of reflection on what was observed. A correction guide listing the expected elements supports consistent assessment.

It is especially useful in natural sciences, geography, environmental education, local history and any subject that uses the environment as a source of learning. Its value lies in the fact that evidence is generated on the spot, providing authenticity and contextualisation that are hard to replicate in the classroom.

**Suitable for:** In situ observation, data recording, connection with the natural or social environment.

**Active techniques using this:** Inquiry-Based Learning, Assessment Target, Listener Teams, Flipped Classroom, One for All

### 85. Educational micro-video `EVI_MICROVIDEO`

_Keywords: micro-video, short, reel, educational, digital_

An educational micro-video is a short audiovisual production (usually between 60 and 90 seconds) in which the student explains a concept, argues a position, summarises a topic or communicates results in a concise and direct way. It differs from a conventional video (EVI_VIDEO) in that it requires maximum condensation of the message; and from a video tutorial (EVI_TUTORIAL) in that it does not necessarily follow a step-by-step procedural structure.

How to use it for assessment: clarity and accuracy of the message, suitability for the intended audience, narrative structure within the limited duration, correctness of content and basic technical quality (image, sound) are all assessed. An analytical rubric that distinguishes between content and production allows specific feedback. Peer assessment is particularly natural for this format.

It is motivating for students who learn better through visual and oral formats than through writing. It develops digital competence, communication skills and the ability to synthesise.

**Suitable for:** Synthesis, concise communication, creativity, digital competence.

**Active techniques using this:** Apply Knowledge, Detect Patterns, Cooperative Graffiti, RallyRobin, RoundRobin

_Keywords: ai, prompts, process, authorship, sources, versions_

The AI process log is evidence in which the student documents how they produced a task when digital tools, external sources or generative AI could be used. It records the work journey, not only the final product.

How to use it for assessment: students record relevant steps, sources consulted, prompts or instructions used, responses used or discarded, changes between versions, checks carried out and their own decisions. They do not need to keep everything, only enough evidence to explain the process.

It supports assessment of authorship, critical thinking, revision, improvement and responsible AI use. It should be linked to specific criteria so that it does not become a formal declaration with no assessment value.

### 87. Response cards / mini-whiteboards `EVI_TARJ_RESP`

_Immediate-response evidence in which the whole group answers at once with cards (A/B/C/D), thumbs or individual mini-whiteboards, giving the teacher an instant snapshot of understanding._

**Type:** Observation · **Phase:** Proceso · **Participation:** Docente · **Complexity:** Baja · **Modality:** Presencial · **Location:** Aula · **Grouping:** Gran grupo · **AI resistance:** Alta

_Keywords: response cards, mini-whiteboard, immediate response, participation_

Response cards and mini-whiteboards are supports for collecting the whole class’s simultaneous answer to a question. Instead of only a few responding, every student shows their answer at once —a card with a letter or colour, thumbs up/down, or what they wrote on their individual whiteboard— so the teacher gets a picture of the whole group’s understanding within seconds.

How it gathers evidence: the teacher poses a closed or short-answer question, gives a few seconds of thinking time and, on a signal, everyone shows their answer. The pattern of responses (how many are right, which error dominates) is the evidence: it allows deciding on the spot whether to move on, re-explain or regroup. It can be recorded occasionally with a participation scale or log.

Its value is immediacy and universal participation: it makes learning visible in real time, keeps every student active and is highly resistant to AI use because it happens in the classroom in real time. It provides formative and diagnostic evidence rather than grading evidence.

**Suitable for:** Quick comprehension checks, hinge questions, review, activating prior knowledge.

**Active techniques using this:** Traffic Light, Peer Instruction, Hinge questions, All-student response

### 88. Storyboard `EVI_STORYBOARD`

_A sequence of frames that visually plans a video, animation or digital story: each panel sketches the image, the action and the text or audio, before producing the final piece._

**Type:** Visual production · **Phase:** Proceso · **Participation:** Docente/Iguales · **Complexity:** Media · **Modality:** Presencial / Online · **Location:** Aula / Virtual · **Grouping:** Individual / Grupo pequeño · **AI resistance:** Media

_Keywords: storyboard, visual planning, digital storytelling, pre-production_

The storyboard is a process evidence: a series of ordered panels representing, shot by shot, what an audiovisual or narrative product will look like (a video, an animation, a comic, a digital story) before recording or editing it. Each panel includes a sketch of the image, a note of the action taking place and the text, dialogue or sound that accompanies it.

How it evidences learning: in making it, learners must structure the story or message, sequence the information, make visual-communication decisions and anticipate the result. The teacher can assess the planning (narrative coherence, clarity, fit to the goal) before time is invested in production, giving early feedback.

Its value is making the thinking that precedes production visible and separating planning from technical execution. It is especially useful in digital-storytelling, video and audiovisual-communication projects, and connects visual thinking with digital competence.

**Suitable for:** Digital storytelling, videos and animations, comics, audiovisual communication projects.

**Active techniques using this:** Digital Storytelling, Visual Thinking, Maker learning / STEAM

## Evaluation instruments


### 1. Analytic rubric `INS_RUB_ANA`

_Matrix that assesses separate criteria using described levels._

**Type:** Qualitative assessment/grading · **Phase:** Proceso/Final · **Participation:** Docente/Alumno/Iguales · **Complexity:** Alta · **Modality:** Presencial / Online · **Location:** Aula / Virtual · **Grouping:** Individual · **AI resistance:** Media

_Keywords: rubric, analytic_

The analytic rubric is a matrix that breaks the task down into separate criteria and describes the performance levels for each. Each criterion is assessed independently, allowing precise feedback on which aspects are strong and which need improvement.

How to use it: design a table where rows are the criteria (e.g., 'structure', 'argumentation', 'linguistic accuracy') and columns are the levels (e.g., 'excellent', 'satisfactory', 'developing', 'beginning'). Each cell contains a descriptor explaining what performance at that level looks like for that criterion. When assessing, the teacher reads each row and marks the level that best describes what was observed.

It is the most suitable assessment tool for complex tasks where detailed feedback matters. It requires design time, but once created it can be reused and shared with students as a guide.

**Purpose:** Assessing complex tasks with multiple aspects.

**Suitable for:** Projects, reports, presentations, debates, digital products.

**Limitations:** Requires design time and good descriptors.

**Example:**

Analytic rubric for assessing an oral presentation in Year 10. Each criterion is scored independently.
|
| Criterion
| Excellent (4)
| Satisfactory (3)
| Developing (2)
| Beginning (1)
| Content
| Clear, well-developed ideas supported by evidence.
| Correct ideas with some details underdeveloped.
| Basic ideas; lacks depth or precision.
| Incomplete or inaccurate information.
| Structure
| Introduction, development and conclusion very clearly distinguished.
| Clear structure with some transitions that could be improved.
| Structure present but poorly defined.
| No appreciable structure.
| Oral expression
| Excellent diction, volume and pace; constant eye contact.
| Fluent delivery with occasional hesitation.
| Functional delivery but with frequent pauses or low voice.
| Significant difficulties in oral communication.
| Time management
| Fits the allotted time perfectly.
| Slight overrun or underrun (±1 min).
| 2–3 minute discrepancy.
| Does not manage the allotted time.
The final mark is obtained by adding the score for each criterion (maximum 16 points) and converting it to a 1–10 scale.

**Active techniques using this:** SOLO Taxonomy, Authentic Assessment, Assessment Rubric, Peer Review, Computational thinking, Fishbone diagram (Ishikawa), Venn diagram

### 2. Global or holistic rubric `INS_RUB_HOL`

_Rubric that assesses performance holistically._

**Type:** Qualitative assessment/grading · **Phase:** Final · **Participation:** Docente · **Complexity:** Media · **Modality:** Presencial / Online · **Location:** Aula / Virtual · **Grouping:** Individual · **AI resistance:** Media

_Keywords: rubric, holistic_

The holistic rubric assesses performance globally, offering a description of each level that covers the task as a whole without separating individual criteria. The assessor selects the level that best describes the work in its entirety.

How to use it: write 3–5 global descriptions ordered from highest to lowest quality. When assessing, read the complete work and select the description that best fits. It is faster than the analytic rubric but offers less diagnostic information.

Suitable when time is limited, when the task is short, or when a quick overall assessment is needed. For work where detailed feedback is a priority, the analytic rubric is more appropriate.

**Purpose:** Assessing a complete task without separating all criteria.

**Suitable for:** Overall products, brief performances, final assessments.

**Limitations:** Offers less diagnostic information.

**Active techniques using this:** Project-Based Learning (PjBL), Challenge-Based Learning (ChBL), Design Thinking, Service-Learning

### 3. Process rubric `INS_RUB_PROC`

_Rubric focused on planning, monitoring, revision and improvement._

**Type:** Formative assessment · **Phase:** Proceso · **Participation:** Docente/Alumno · **Complexity:** Alta · **Modality:** Presencial / Online · **Location:** Aula / Virtual · **Grouping:** Individual · **AI resistance:** Alta

_Keywords: rubric, process_

The process rubric focuses assessment on how the task is developed: planning, organisation, revisions, time management, decision-making and use of resources. It does not assess the final product, but the path taken to reach it.

How to use it: criteria include aspects such as 'defines clear objectives at the outset', 'records progress and difficulties', 'reviews and adjusts the plan when necessary' or 'manages the group's time well'. It is applied during the development of the project, not at the end.

It is essential in long-duration projects, research and learning portfolios, where the learning process is as valuable as the result. It enables difficulties to be identified early and feedback to be given while there is still time to act.

**Purpose:** Assessing how a task is developed.

**Suitable for:** Projects, research, learning portfolios, project logs.

**Limitations:** Requires ongoing monitoring.

**Active techniques using this:** Project-Based Learning (PjBL), Challenge-Based Learning (ChBL), Design Thinking, Service-Learning, Maker learning / STEAM, Genius Hour, ICAP framework

### 4. Product rubric `INS_RUB_PROD`

_Rubric focused on the quality of the final product._

**Type:** Assessment/grading · **Phase:** Final · **Participation:** Docente · **Complexity:** Media/Alta · **Modality:** Presencial / Online · **Location:** Aula / Virtual · **Grouping:** Individual · **AI resistance:** Media

_Keywords: rubric, product_

The product rubric evaluates the characteristics of the final artefact submitted: quality, structure, content, presentation, coherence and compliance with requirements. Unlike the process rubric, it does not consider how the result was achieved.

How to use it: criteria focus on the product itself: 'the information is accurate and relevant', 'the structure is clear and coherent', 'the format is appropriate for the audience'. Each criterion has described levels that allow the work to be located.

It complements the process rubric in extended projects. Used alone, it may lead to assessing only the result and not the learning.

**Purpose:** Assessing the final result.

**Suitable for:** Written, digital, visual or physical products.

**Limitations:** May leave the process out if used alone.

**Active techniques using this:** Project-Based Learning (PjBL), Challenge-Based Learning (ChBL), Design Thinking, Dual coding, Service-Learning, WebQuest, Maker learning / STEAM, Mind map, Timeline

### 5. Oral rubric `INS_RUB_ORAL`

During the performance, the assessor marks the level of each criterion while observing. If there are many students, the performance can be recorded for later review (with the necessary permissions).

Sharing it before the performance allows students to prepare more effectively and reduces anxiety by knowing exactly what will be assessed.

**Purpose:** Assessing oral communication, content and interaction.

**Suitable for:** Oral performances.

**Limitations:** Can be difficult to record everything live.

### 7. Competency rubric `INS_RUB_COMP`

The competency rubric is designed to assess the degree of development of a specific competency (communicative, mathematical, digital, scientific, etc.) through a contextualised task. Its criteria are aligned with the operational descriptors of the educational level.

### 8. Checklist `INS_LISTA`

_List of indicators with presence or absence._

**Type:** Record/assessment · **Phase:** Inicial/Proceso/Final · **Participation:** Docente/Alumno/Iguales · **Complexity:** Baja · **Modality:** Presencial / Online · **Location:** Aula / Virtual · **Grouping:** Individual · **AI resistance:** Media

_Keywords: checklist, list_

The checklist is an assessment tool that lists the elements, steps or behaviours that must be present in a task or performance. For each item, the assessor simply marks whether it is present (yes/no) or has been fulfilled.

How to use it: draw up a list of concrete, observable indicators (e.g., 'includes an introduction', 'cites sources', 'uses safety equipment'). During or after the activity, mark each item. There are no intermediate levels; it is a binary check.

Fast to apply and very useful for activities with clear, closed requirements: laboratory protocols, presentations with defined requirements, technical procedures. For tasks with qualitative nuances, a rubric or scale is preferable.

**Purpose:** Checking requirements, steps or specific evidence.

**Suitable for:** Simple tasks or clear procedures.

**Limitations:** Does not assess quality or nuance well.

**Active techniques using this:** Laboratory-Based Learning, Problem-Based Learning (PBL), Project-Based Learning (PjBL), Challenge-Based Learning (ChBL), Design Thinking, Flipped Classroom, Gallery Walk, Generate Solutions, Service-Learning, One for All, Peer Review, Question Starts

### 9. Observation scale `INS_ESC_OBS`

_Scale for assessing frequency, intensity or degree of a performance._

**Type:** Record/assessment · **Phase:** Proceso · **Participation:** Docente · **Complexity:** Media · **Modality:** Presencial / Online · **Location:** Aula / Virtual · **Grouping:** Individual · **AI resistance:** Alta

_Keywords: scale, observation_

The observation scale records the degree to which a behaviour, attitude or skill is displayed during an activity. Unlike the checklist (yes/no), it offers several points on the scale (e.g., always / almost always / sometimes / rarely / never).

How to use it: define the indicators to be observed and choose a frequency or quality scale. During the activity, the teacher marks the observed level for each student. It can be designed to observe several students at once if the indicators are few and specific.

Useful for assessing participation, attitudes, procedures and social skills in group activities, debates, practical activities and workshops.

**Purpose:** Recording observable performances.

**Suitable for:** Participation, cooperative work, practical activities, presentations.

**Limitations:** Can be ambiguous without clear descriptors.

### 10. Rating scale `INS_ESC_VAL`

_Graduated scale for assessing degree of achievement._

**Type:** Assessment · **Phase:** Proceso/Final · **Participation:** Docente/Alumno/Iguales · **Complexity:** Media · **Modality:** Presencial / Online · **Location:** Aula / Virtual · **Grouping:** Individual · **AI resistance:** Media

_Keywords: scale_

The rating scale assigns a numerical or qualitative value to each criterion assessed, without reaching the precision of a rubric in describing what each level means. It is an intermediate point between the checklist and the analytic rubric.

How to use it: list the criteria and place a scale next to each one (e.g., 1–4 or insufficient/sufficient/good/excellent). The assessor assigns a value to each criterion based on their judgement. It is faster than a rubric because it does not require detailed descriptors for each cell.

Suitable when the teacher has enough experience to apply criteria implicitly, but may be less reliable and less informative for students than a rubric with descriptors.

**Purpose:** Assessing achievements with levels.

**Suitable for:** Tasks of low or medium complexity.

**Limitations:** Less precise than a full rubric.

**Active techniques using this:** What makes you say that?, Game-Based Learning (GBL), Problem-Based Learning (PBL), Structured Academic Controversy, The Sack of Doubts, Academic debate

### 11. Descriptive scale `INS_ESC_DESC`

_Scale whose levels include descriptions._

**Type:** Qualitative assessment · **Phase:** Proceso/Final · **Participation:** Docente · **Complexity:** Media · **Modality:** Presencial / Online · **Location:** Aula / Virtual · **Grouping:** Individual · **AI resistance:** Media

_Keywords: descriptive_

The descriptive scale is a variant of the rating scale in which each point on the scale is accompanied by a verbal description explaining what characterises that level. It is more precise than the numerical scale but less detailed than the analytic rubric.

How to use it: for each criterion, write 3–4 descriptions ordered from highest to lowest quality. When assessing, read the description that best corresponds to the observed work. It can be combined with a numerical score associated with each level.

It is a good starting point for teachers who want to introduce more explicit criteria without investing the time required to design a full analytic rubric.

**Purpose:** Assessing quality with greater clarity than a numerical scale.

**Suitable for:** Open tasks of medium complexity.

**Limitations:** Requires writing descriptors.

**Active techniques using this:** What makes you say that?, Game-Based Learning (GBL), Problem-Based Learning (PBL), Structured Academic Controversy, The Sack of Doubts

### 12. Correction guide `INS_GUIA_CORR`

_Document with criteria, expected answers or marking guidelines._

**Type:** Correction/grading · **Phase:** Final · **Participation:** Docente · **Complexity:** Media · **Modality:** Presencial / Online · **Location:** Aula / Virtual · **Grouping:** Individual · **AI resistance:** Media

_Keywords: guide, marking_

The correction guide specifies what should be found in a correct or quality response: which concepts, which arguments, which steps, which elements. It guides the marking of written, oral or practical tests.

How to use it: before marking, the teacher draws up a guide listing the expected points in each question or task, indicating how many marks each is worth. When marking, the student's response is compared with the guide and the corresponding score is assigned. It can include examples of acceptable responses or common errors.

It increases the consistency of marking across different marking sessions by the same teacher or across different teachers marking the same exam.

**Purpose:** Marking tests or tasks with common criteria.

**Suitable for:** Tests, commentaries, problems, reports.

**Limitations:** Less suitable for very open tasks if not adapted.

**Active techniques using this:** Inquiry-Based Learning, Problem-Based Learning (PBL), Group Investigation, Word-Phrase-Sentence, Concrete examples

### 13. Correction template `INS_PLANT_CORR`

_Format for recording scores or correct answers._

**Type:** Correction/grading · **Phase:** Inicial/Final · **Participation:** Docente · **Complexity:** Baja · **Modality:** Presencial / Online · **Location:** Aula / Virtual · **Grouping:** Individual · **AI resistance:** Media

_Keywords: template_

The correction template is a structured document that organises the marking task: it lists the questions or tasks, indicates the maximum score for each, and provides space to record the score obtained and observations.

How to use it: it is prepared before marking and used as a recording sheet during the process. It can include model answers, general criteria and notes on typical errors detected during marking.

Especially useful when there are many tests to mark or when several teachers are marking the same instrument, as it guarantees uniformity and speeds up the process.

**Purpose:** Marking quickly and consistently.

**Suitable for:** Objective tests or short-answer tests.

**Limitations:** Can reduce assessment to correct answers.

**Active techniques using this:** Inquiry-Based Learning, Problem-Based Learning (PBL), Group Investigation, Word-Phrase-Sentence

### 14. Scoring scale `INS_BAREMO`

_Distribution of points among sections or indicators._

**Type:** Grading · **Phase:** Final · **Participation:** Docente · **Complexity:** Media · **Modality:** Presencial / Online · **Location:** Aula / Virtual · **Grouping:** Individual · **AI resistance:** Media

_Keywords: scoring scale_

The scoring scale is a scoring system that establishes how much each part of a test, task or set of criteria is worth, and how partial scores are translated into an overall mark.

How to use it: define the maximum score for each question, exercise or criterion, and indicate the formula for calculating the final mark (direct sum, weighting, rounding). If there is a penalty for errors, it must be clearly specified. The scoring scale should be made public before the test so that students know how much each part is worth.

A well-designed scoring scale reflects the relative importance of each content area or competency assessed and facilitates objective grading.

**Purpose:** Assigning scores.

**Suitable for:** Tests and tasks with defined scoring.

**Limitations:** Can become mechanical without qualitative criteria.

**Active techniques using this:** Inquiry-Based Learning, Problem-Based Learning (PBL), Group Investigation, Word-Phrase-Sentence

### 15. Anecdotal record `INS_REG_ANE`

_Brief note of significant observed events._

**Type:** Qualitative record · **Phase:** Proceso · **Participation:** Docente · **Complexity:** Baja/Media · **Modality:** Presencial · **Location:** Aula / Laboratorio / Exterior · **Grouping:** Individual · **AI resistance:** Alta

_Keywords: anecdotal_

The anecdotal record is a document where the teacher briefly and with dates notes significant events observed in students: notable progress, a persistent difficulty, outstanding behaviour, a relevant contribution.

How to use it: it can be a notebook, a card per student, an app or a sheet divided by student. The key is to record the event as observed (without interpreting it yet) and add the date. Over time, the accumulation of anecdotes reveals patterns of learning or behaviour that would otherwise go unnoticed.

Especially useful in stages without numerical grading, for completing descriptive reports, guiding tutorials and grounding continuous formative assessment.

**Purpose:** Collecting relevant evidence not always anticipated.

**Suitable for:** Behaviours, progress, difficulties, incidents.

**Limitations:** Not sufficient on its own for grading.

**Active techniques using this:** Problem-Based Learning (PBL), Project-Based Learning (PjBL), CO-OP CO-OP, Structured Academic Controversy, The Sack of Doubts, Metacognition Ladder, Flipped Classroom

### 16. Descriptive record `INS_REG_DESC`

_Detailed record of performances, processes or behaviours._

**Type:** Qualitative record · **Phase:** Proceso · **Participation:** Docente · **Complexity:** Media · **Modality:** Presencial / Online · **Location:** Aula / Virtual · **Grouping:** Individual · **AI resistance:** Alta

_Keywords: descriptive_

The descriptive record is a narrative assessment instrument in which the teacher describes in some detail the performance of a student or group during an activity or period, without reducing it to a score.

How to use it: write a qualitative description explaining what the student did, how they did it and what evidence was observed. It can be structured by criteria or by moments in the activity. It is more elaborate than the anecdotal record and more appropriate for periodic reports.

It allows families and the student themselves to receive a rich and nuanced picture of learning, beyond what a numerical grade can convey.

**Purpose:** Documenting observable evidence in detail.

**Suitable for:** Complex processes or individual monitoring.

**Limitations:** Time-consuming.

**Active techniques using this:** Problem-Based Learning (PBL), Project-Based Learning (PjBL), CO-OP CO-OP, Structured Academic Controversy, The Sack of Doubts, Metacognition Ladder, Flipped Classroom

### 41. Level-to-grade converter `INS_CONV`

**Type:** Grading · **Phase:** Final · **Participation:** Docente · **Complexity:** Media · **Modality:** Presencial / Online · **Location:** Aula / Virtual · **Grouping:** Individual · **AI resistance:** Media

_Keywords: conversion_

The level-to-grade converter is a table or formula that translates the qualitative levels of a rubric or descriptive scale into a numerical grade, facilitating the grading process when the curriculum requires a numerical mark.

### 59. Table of specifications `INS_TAB_ESPEC`

The table of specifications (or test blueprint) is a design tool created before writing a test. ### 60. Single-point rubric `INS_RUB_PUNTO`

_A rubric that describes only the target level (the expected performance) for each criterion, leaving space to note where work falls short and where it exceeds it. It simplifies design and prioritises feedback._

**Type:** Qualitative assessment · **Phase:** Proceso/Final · **Participation:** Docente/Alumno/Iguales · **Complexity:** Media · **Modality:** Presencial / Online · **Location:** Aula / Virtual · **Grouping:** Individual · **AI resistance:** Media

_Keywords: rubric, single-point, feedback, qualitative_

The single-point rubric is a simplified variant of the analytic rubric. Instead of describing every level of performance for each criterion, it describes only the target level (what is expected) in a central column, and reserves a column on the left to note where the work falls short and another on the right to note where it exceeds expectations.

How to use it: write the criteria and, for each, only the descriptor of the expected level. When marking, the teacher (or the student or a peer) writes concrete comments in the “not yet there” and “exceeds” columns, personalising the feedback instead of ticking a box.

Its value is that it is quick to design, does not box learners into predefined levels and shifts the focus towards qualitative feedback and improvement. It is especially suitable for self- and peer assessment and for open or creative tasks.

**Purpose:** Assess criteria by describing only the expected level and focusing marking on feedback.

**Suitable for:** Essays, projects, open or creative tasks, self- and peer assessment.

**Limitations:** Provides less detail than an analytic rubric and depends on the quality of written comments.

**Active techniques using this:** Project-Based Learning (PjBL), Portfolio (learning dossier), Authentic Assessment, Assessment Rubric, Peer Review

### 61. Ladder of feedback `INS_ESCAL_FB`

_A structured feedback protocol in four rungs —clarify, value, ask questions and suggest— that orders comments so they are respectful, specific and useful, especially among peers._

**Type:** Feedback · **Phase:** Proceso · **Participation:** Docente/Iguales/Alumno · **Complexity:** Baja/Media · **Modality:** Presencial / Online · **Location:** Aula / Virtual · **Grouping:** Grupo pequeño · **AI resistance:** Alta

_Keywords: feedback, ladder of feedback, peer assessment, Project Zero_

The ladder of feedback, proposed within the Project Zero framework, is a protocol that organises feedback into a sequence of four steps so that it is constructive and does not turn into vague or hurtful criticism.

The four rungs are: 1) Clarify (ask questions to understand well what has been done, avoiding assumptions); 2) Value (point out strengths and what works specifically); 3) Ask questions (raise doubts and concerns as questions, not judgements); and 4) Suggest (offer concrete ideas for improvement).

How to use it: before a peer-review session or a discussion of a piece of work, the ladder is presented and comments are asked to follow that order. It works very well with a script or template providing sentence starters for each rung. Its value is to create a safe climate for feedback, improve the quality of comments and teach learners to give and receive feedback.

**Purpose:** Structure feedback into steps (clarify, value, ask, suggest) so it is useful and respectful.

**Suitable for:** Peer review, commenting on drafts, project critique sessions.

**Limitations:** Requires practice and prior modelling so it does not become a mechanical formality.

**Active techniques using this:** Design Thinking, Peer Review, Two Stars and a Wish

### 62. Self-regulation guide (base of orientation) `INS_BASE_ORIENT`

_Keywords: base of orientation, assessment as learning, self-regulation, scaffolding_

The base of orientation is an instrument of formative-as-learning assessment: a guide, ideally built with the learners, that sets out in order the steps, operations and criteria needed to perform a task or procedure well (solving a problem, writing an argumentative text, carrying out a lab practical).

How it is applied: after jointly analysing how a task is done well, a list or diagram is built with the key actions and the points where it is worth pausing. Learners keep it in front of them while working and use it to guide and review their own process; over time they internalise it until they no longer need it. It also works as an instrument for self-assessment and error detection.

Its value is developing autonomy and self-regulation: it hands learners control of the process, makes the “how to do it” explicit and reduces dependence on the teacher. It is especially useful for complex procedures and for attention to diversity.

**Purpose:** Guide the execution and self-regulation of a task through its key steps and criteria.

**Suitable for:** Problem solving, lab procedures, structured writing, complex tasks.

**Limitations:** If handed over ready-made instead of built with learners, it may be used mechanically.

## Assessment dimensions


### 1. Self-assessment `DIM_AUTO`

_Students' assessment of their own learning._

**Category:** Agente evaluador · **Phase:** Inicial/Proceso/Final · **Participation:** Alumno · **Modality:** Presencial / Online · **Location:** Aula / Virtual · **Grouping:** Individual · **AI resistance:** Baja

_Keywords: self-assessment, metacognition_

This dimension indicates who participates in the assessment of evidence. It does not determine the instrument on its own, but rather the role of the teacher, the students or peers in the assessment process.

Self-assessment is the process by which students assess their own learning, their work process or the result they have produced, using previously established and understood criteria.

How to apply it: it is not about asking for a free opinion ('did you enjoy it?'), but about providing a scale, rubric or list of specific indicators and asking students to apply them honestly to their own performance. It is advisable that they have previously seen examples of how those criteria are applied and discussed what each level means.

Its main value is metacognitive: it requires students to reflect on what they know, what they find difficult and how they can improve. When combined with the teacher's assessment, it allows discrepancies to be identified and self-regulation to be developed.

**When to use:** When students are expected to become aware of their own progress.

**Typical evidence:** Reflections, scales, journals, self-reports, KPSI.

**Pedagogic function:** Foster metacognition, autonomy and self-regulation.

**Active techniques using this:** Challenge-Based Learning (ChBL), Assessment Target, Learning Journal, 5E Instructional Model, Traffic Light, Metacognition Ladder, Portfolio (learning dossier), Distribute Points, Assessment Rubric, Self-explanation, Genius Hour

### 2. Peer assessment `DIM_COEV`

_Assessment of peers' performance or product using shared criteria._

**Category:** Agente evaluador · **Phase:** Proceso/Final · **Participation:** Iguales · **Modality:** Presencial / Online · **Location:** Aula / Virtual · **Grouping:** Parejas / Grupo pequeño · **AI resistance:** Media

_Keywords: peer assessment, peers_

This dimension indicates who participates in the assessment of evidence. It does not determine the instrument on its own, but rather the role of the teacher, the students or peers in the assessment process.

Peer assessment is the assessment students make of the work or performance of their classmates, using shared criteria. It is a form of assessment among peers that, when well designed, improves understanding of criteria and develops critical thinking.

How to apply it: criteria must be clear, specific and have been worked on beforehand. It is recommended to start with tasks where assessing others' work is relatively straightforward (e.g. checking whether an outline includes the required elements) before moving on to more complex assessments. To reduce friendship bias, it can be done anonymously or in a cross-assessment format.

Its main function is formative: peer comments, combined with clear criteria, help to improve work before the final submission. It should only contribute to grading when students have sufficient practice and confidence in the process.

**When to use:** When introducing peer assessment and draft improvement.

**Typical evidence:** Comments, reviews, scales, rubrics between peers.

**Pedagogic function:** Improve work, understand criteria and learn among peers.

**Active techniques using this:** CO-OP CO-OP, Assessment Target, Fishbowl (Aquarium), Gallery Walk, Exchange difficulties, Distribute Points, Assessment Rubric, Peer Review, Two Stars and a Wish, Fan-N-Pick, Three-Step Interview

### 18. Feedback and feedforward `DIM_FEED`

**Category:** Finalidad y uso · **Phase:** Proceso · **Participation:** Docente/Alumno/Iguales · **Modality:** Presencial / Online · **Location:** Aula / Virtual · **Grouping:** Individual / Grupo pequeño · **AI resistance:** Media

_Keywords: feedback, feedforward, improvement_

Feedback explains to students where they stand in relation to the objective, what they have done well, what they need to improve and what the next step is. Feedforward adds guidance towards future tasks, not just correction of what has already been submitted.

How to apply it: brief comments, cards, individual conferences, draft reviews or guided peer assessment are used. The essential thing is that students can act on the information received.

It has more impact when it arrives during the process, focuses on specific criteria and avoids generic messages such as 'well done' or 'you need to try harder'.

**When to use:** When students can revise, repeat, improve or transfer what they have learned to a subsequent task.

**Typical evidence:** Comments, feedback cards, improvement commitments, draft reviews.

**Pedagogic function:** Turn assessment into concrete improvement action.

**Active techniques using this:** Analyze and Synthesize, Game-Based Learning (GBL), Question-Based Learning (QBL), Problem-Based Learning (PBL), Project-Based Learning (PjBL), Challenge-Based Learning (ChBL), Chain of Questions, CO-OP CO-OP, Compare and Contrast, Learning Contract, Structured Academic Controversy, Timed Pair Share, Design Thinking, Assessment Target, Learning Journal, Roundtable, 5E Instructional Model, The Sack of Doubts, Traffic Light, Listener Teams, Metacognition Ladder, Learning Stations, Fishbowl (Aquarium), Flipped Classroom, Gallery Walk, Generate Hypotheses, Exchange difficulties, Role Play, KWL (I know, I want to know, I have learned), Compass Points, Pencils in the Middle, Ghost Reading, 5 Whys, Minute Paper, Muddiest Point, Numbered Heads Together, Word-Phrase-Sentence, Three-Minute Pause, Detective Pairs, Peer Instruction, Portfolio (learning dossier), Ask and Answer, Service-Learning, Quiz-Quiz-Trade, RallyRobin, Distribute Points, RoundRobin, STAD (Student Teams Achievement Divisions), Exit Ticket, One for All, Understanding by Design (UbD), Depth of Knowledge (DOK), SOLO Taxonomy, Bloom's Taxonomy, Authentic Assessment, Visible Learning, SAMR Model, Universal Design for Learning (UDL), Peer Tutoring, Team Assisted Individualization (TAI), Co-teaching, Gamification, Assessment Rubric, Cooperative Roles, Claim, Evidence and Reasoning (CER), Argument-Driven Inquiry (ADI), Predict, Observe, Explain (POE), Toulmin's Argumentation Model, Peer Review, Two Stars and a Wish, Argument Map, Structured Controversy with Evidence, Retrieval practice, Spaced practice, Mastery learning, Orientation guides, +1, Cornell notes, One-sentence summary

### 19. Grading and accreditation `DIM_CAL_ACRED`

_Justified transformation of evidence into a grade, promotion or certification decision._

**Category:** Finalidad y uso · **Phase:** Final · **Participation:** Docente/Equipo docente · **Modality:** Presencial / Online · **Location:** Aula / Virtual · **Grouping:** Individual · **AI resistance:** Media

_Keywords: grading, accreditation, mark, promotion_

Grading and accreditation convert learning evidence into a communicable decision: a grade, a level of achievement, a promotion or a certification.

How to apply it: it is made explicit which criteria count, what evidence supports them and how they are combined. Criteria grading tables, weighting matrices, records and individual reports help to make the decision traceable.

It is a necessary function of the educational system, but must be distinguished from formative feedback: grading informs about the level achieved; guiding improvement requires additional information.

**When to use:** When closing units, assessments, courses or processes requiring an administrative decision.

**Typical evidence:** Tests, final tasks, rubrics, criteria tables, records and reports.

**Pedagogic function:** Communicate the level achieved and support academic decisions with evidence.

**Active techniques using this:** Learning Contract, Portfolio (learning dossier), Distribute Points, STAD (Student Teams Achievement Divisions), Teams-Games-Tournament (TGT), Understanding by Design (UbD), Authentic Assessment, Assessment Rubric, Mastery learning

### 20. Shared and participatory assessment `DIM_COMPART`

_Student participation in criteria, evidence, review and improvement decisions._

**Category:** Agente evaluador · **Phase:** Proceso/Final · **Participation:** Docente/Alumno/Iguales · **Modality:** Presencial / Online · **Location:** Aula / Virtual · **Grouping:** Individual / Gran grupo · **AI resistance:** Media

_Keywords: shared, participatory, peer assessment, negotiation_

Shared and participatory assessment involves students in different parts of the assessment process: understanding or constructing criteria, reviewing evidence, carrying out peer assessment, self-assessing and proposing improvements.

How to apply it: indicators can be agreed upon, group contracts used, drafts reviewed by peers, or self-assessment compared with the teacher's assessment. The final responsibility for grading remains with the teacher when the system requires it.

It is especially valuable because it makes the criteria visible and turns assessment into learning about the quality of one's own work.

**When to use:** In projects, cooperative work, portfolios and tasks where students can review their process.

**Typical evidence:** Criteria negotiation, peer assessment, group contracts, improvement commitments.

**Pedagogic function:** Increase understanding of criteria, responsibility and self-regulation.

**Active techniques using this:** CO-OP CO-OP, Learning Contract, Assessment Target, Fishbowl (Aquarium), Gallery Walk, Exchange difficulties, Round Table, Portfolio (learning dossier), Distribute Points, Authentic Assessment, The 4Cs of Learning, Assessment Rubric, Peer Review, Two Stars and a Wish, Academic debate, Chalk Talk, Community of Inquiry

### 21. Gamified assessment `DIM_GAMIF`

_Use of game mechanics to collect evidence of learning._

**Category:** Contexto metodológico · **Phase:** Proceso/Final · **Participation:** Docente/Alumno/Iguales · **Modality:** Presencial / Online · **Location:** Classroom / Virtual · **Grouping:** Individual / Small group / Whole class · **AI resistance:** Media

_Keywords: gamification, game-based, escape room, challenge, badges_

This dimension describes the didactic format or situation in which evidence is generated. It can combine several collection procedures.

Gamified assessment incorporates game mechanics (challenges, points, badges, countdowns, regulated cooperation or competition) to collect evidence of learning in a highly motivating environment. It is not synonymous with a specific instrument such as an escape room (EVI_ESCAPE), but rather the methodological approach that encompasses any strategy using games for assessment purposes.

How to apply it: tasks or challenges are designed with clear rules, visible achievement criteria and immediate feedback. Points or badges can be used as evidence of the level reached if they are aligned with real assessment criteria. Analytics from the gamified platform (time, attempts, correct answers) complement the teacher's observation but do not replace it.

It is especially suitable for formative assessment, consolidation and diagnostic assessment, as the playful context reduces assessment anxiety and encourages participation from students who struggle with motivation.

**When to use:** When high motivation is sought, when consolidating learning, or when conducting active diagnostic assessment.

**Typical evidence:** Challenges, escape rooms, timed rounds, badges, leaderboards, cooperative quests.

**Pedagogic function:** Increase motivation and engagement, and obtain immediate feedback on learning.

**Active techniques using this:** Game-Based Learning (GBL), Word Game, Role Play, Quiz-Quiz-Trade, Teams-Games-Tournament (TGT), NASA Game, Gamification, Showdown

### 22. Traceability, authorship and AI use `DIM_TRAZ_IA`

_Documentation of the process, sources, external support and AI use behind a piece of evidence._

**Category:** Soporte de evidencias · **Phase:** Proceso/Final · **Participation:** Docente/Alumno · **Modality:** Presencial / Online · **Location:** Aula / Virtual / Domicilio · **Grouping:** Individual / Grupo pequeño · **AI resistance:** Alta

_Keywords: ai, authorship, traceability, sources, process_

This dimension places assessment in contexts where the production process may be hidden or supported by external tools, including generative AI.

How to apply it: students document which sources, supports, tools or AI systems they used, what they used them for and which decisions were their own. They may keep versions, relevant prompts, changes made, source checks and brief justifications. The teacher assesses the quality of the process, critical review and the match between the final product and the learning demonstrated.

It is especially useful in research tasks, projects, collaborative documents, portfolios, digital products and tasks completed partly outside the classroom. Its purpose is not to penalise AI use, but to make it transparent and assessable.

**When to use:** When a task allows external help, digital editing, work outside the classroom or generative AI use.

**Typical evidence:** Versions, prompts, sources, revision history, justified decisions, AI-use declaration.

**Pedagogic function:** Make the production process visible and distinguish demonstrated learning from the support used.

**Active techniques using this:** Discovery Learning, Getting to Know Each Other, Word-Phrase-Sentence

### 23. Ipsative assessment `DIM_IPSA`

_Assessment that compares learners with themselves: it measures progress relative to their own starting point, not relative to the group norm nor only to a fixed criterion._

**Category:** Enfoque curricular · **Phase:** Proceso/Final · **Participation:** Docente/Alumno · **Modality:** Presencial / Online · **Location:** Aula / Virtual · **Grouping:** Individual · **AI resistance:** Media

_Keywords: ipsative, individual progress, motivation, self-regulation_

Ipsative assessment takes the student’s own previous performance as its reference: what matters is how much they have progressed relative to themselves. It does not compare with the class average (norm-referenced) nor merely against an external standard (criterion-referenced); instead it focuses on individual improvement over time.

How to apply it: a starting point is recorded (a first piece of work, an initial test, a completed rubric) and, at successive moments, the same competence is assessed again with the same criteria, comparing each new piece of evidence with the student’s previous one. Progress is made visible through progress scales, achievement profiles or dashboards showing the evolution.

Its value is motivational and metacognitive: it recognises each student’s effort and improvement from wherever they start, is especially fair to diversity and fosters self-regulation. It should be combined with criterion-referenced assessment so as not to lose sight of the standards.

**When to use:** When you want to assess each student’s improvement relative to their own starting point.

**Typical evidence:** Comparison of successive pieces of work, progress scales, achievement profiles, portfolios.

**Pedagogic function:** Make individual progress visible and strengthen motivation and self-regulation.

**Active techniques using this:** Learning Contract, Assessment Target, Learning Journal, Metacognition Ladder, Portfolio (learning dossier), Distribute Points, STAD (Student Teams Achievement Divisions), Teams-Games-Tournament (TGT), Mastery learning, Personalised learning playlists

### 24. Norm-referenced assessment `DIM_NORM`

_Assessment that interprets each student’s performance by comparing it with the group (the norm): it places the person relative to their peers’ standing._

**Category:** Enfoque curricular · **Phase:** Final · **Participation:** Docente · **Modality:** Presencial / Online · **Location:** Aula / Virtual · **Grouping:** Individual / Gran grupo · **AI resistance:** Media

_Keywords: norm-referenced, norm, scoring norms, comparison_

Norm-referenced assessment interprets results by comparing each student with the performance of the whole group: the reference is the group’s distribution, not an absolute criterion. It answers questions like “are they above or below average?” or “what is their relative standing?”.

How it is applied: the same test is given to the whole group and results are interpreted with norms, percentiles or scales that rank students against one another. It is common in standardised tests and in selection or classification processes.

It is included in the catalogue as a conceptual reference, but with caution: for classroom assessment, criterion-referenced (comparing against achievement criteria) and ipsative (comparing with oneself) assessment are recommended, because norm-referencing can be unfair to diversity and shift the focus of learning towards peer comparison.

**When to use:** When ranking or classification is needed (e.g. standardised tests); not advisable in the classroom.

**Typical evidence:** Standardised tests, norms, percentiles, comparative scales.

**Pedagogic function:** Place individual performance relative to the group’s standing.

**Active techniques using this:** Understanding by Design (UbD), Depth of Knowledge (DOK), Bloom's Taxonomy, Constructive alignment

### 25. Dynamic assessment `DIM_DINAM`

_Assessment that measures learning potential by observing how learners improve when given help during the assessment itself, rather than measuring only what they do unaided._

**Category:** Contexto metodológico · **Phase:** Proceso · **Participation:** Docente/Alumno · **Modality:** Presencial / Online · **Location:** Aula / Virtual · **Grouping:** Individual / Grupo pequeño · **AI resistance:** Media

_Keywords: dynamic, learning potential, mediation, Vygotsky, ZPD_

Dynamic assessment, inspired by Vygotsky’s zone of proximal development and Feuerstein’s work, does not merely record what learners can do on their own but assesses what they can achieve with mediation. It follows a test–mediation–retest sequence: first autonomous performance is observed, then the teacher offers graduated help (hints, questions, models), and finally performance is observed again to see how much and how it has improved.

How it is applied: during a sufficiently challenging task, the teacher intervenes with increasingly explicit supports and records what kind of help the student needs and how they respond to it. The interest lies not only in the result but in the learning process and the potential revealed through mediation.

Its value is both diagnostic and formative: it informs about each student’s zone of proximal development and guides subsequent scaffolding. It requires time and individualised interaction, so it is usually applied occasionally or with small groups.

**When to use:** When you want to know learning potential and what supports learners need, not just the result.

**Typical evidence:** Record of the help needed and of improvement across the test-mediation-retest sequence.

**Pedagogic function:** Reveal learning potential and guide scaffolding based on the response to mediation.

**Active techniques using this:** Peer Tutoring, Reciprocal Teaching, Gradual release of responsibility, Orientation guides, Scaffolding and zone of proximal development (ZPD)
